### comment


The English music hall entertainer George Chirgin (1854-1922), the son of a circus clown, was billed as ‘the white-eyed Kaffir’, a black face minstrel act. Known for his unusual costume and make up, his entire face was blackened but for a white diamond shape surrounding one eye, and he wore an outsized top hat, tights and black cape. He always ended his performances with his signature song, ‘The Blind Boy’, although the words quoted here by Edward Stuart-Humphries are from a different song.

### comment


Cared for initially by foster parents in Exeter, most of the childhood that Edward Stewart-Humphries (b. 1889) recalls is with his biological parents in Plymouth, and then London. The memoir ends in 1906 when he relocates to Scotland to start his army career with the Ist Battalion Royal Scots Regiment. The unpublished memoir is held in the Burnett Archive of Working Class Autobiographies, Brunel University Library, Special Collections.

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">[Edward Stewart-Humphries and his mother relocated from Cornwall to London in 1903. He worked as a page boy and other similar jobs at hotels and private clubs, visiting London Music Halls on a weekly basis]&nbsp;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><strong><span style="font-size: 11pt;">&nbsp;</span></strong></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">My memory [of Music Hall entertainments] lingers most, however, on George Gershwin&nbsp;[<em>sic</em>]<em>,</em>the White-eyed Kaffir, a speciality turn with his long one-string fiddle. He invariably ended his act by singing the same tiny ditty which he accompanied on his fiddle. Indeed, he was not allowed to depart until, with due ceremony he had seated himself on a stool with fiddle resting on the floor; lights out except for a strong beam of light centred on his white-eyed black face: then to the sadly sweet music which he conjured from his lengthy fiddle he sang:</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman'; padding-left: 90px;"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">&ldquo;My fiddle is my sweetheart, yes!</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman'; padding-left: 90px;"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">And I&rsquo;m her faithful beau (bow).</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman'; padding-left: 90px;"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">I hug her close to my bosom, yes,</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman'; padding-left: 90px;"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">Because I love her so!&rdquo;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">The poignant sweetness he produced with his controlled tenor-falsetto voice in harmony with his fiddle was out of this world and endeared him for ever in all our memories. Unless my memory fails me, he was a great favourite at the old Middlesex in Drury Lane, then known as &ldquo;the Old Mo&rdquo;.&nbsp;</span></p>
