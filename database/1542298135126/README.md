### comment


Richard W. Morris (born 1895) wrote his memoir in 1972 in response to a call from the Beamish Living History Museum for local stories and memoirs. It focuses on his childhood in County Durham colliery villages and on the poverty, hardships and skills of coal mining. Extracts from the untitled memoir are published as “A Boy Goes Down the Pit’ in Bulletin of the County Local History Society, no. 20, Oct. 1977, pp. 4-12 (edited by G. Patterson).

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0cm 0cm 0.0001pt; font-size: medium; font-family: 'Times New Roman';"><span style="font-size: 11pt;">It will not be out of place to mentioned [<em>sic</em>] the great and wonderful mighty Showmens&rsquo; Steam Engines. [&hellip;] These lovely pieces of machinery with their steam and other mechanical organs, used to thrill us young lads to bits [&hellip;] I used to be amazed at the way they set about the task of getting their tackle out of the Main Street of Chester-le-Street up into the market fair ground. [...] They used all sorts of tricks to get the wheels to grip in all that slimy mud. I have seen all kinds of wooden boxes and crates crammed under the wheels, and ashes, sacks and planks of wood, anything to get those great engines, and their loads, up into the market. [&hellip;] As a very young lad I used to be enthralled with their mechanical organs and blaring music, and at the antics of all the mechanical figures. These are now [1972] almost priceless antiques, but to people of my generation they bring back some nostalgic memories.</span></p>
